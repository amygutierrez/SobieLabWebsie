Research

Combining systems pharmacology modeling with machine learning techniques to identify subgroups at risk of an arrhythmia

Cardiac cell susceptibility based on potassium channel diffrences are one of our areas of research. Slow and rapid potassium channels delayed rectifiers are studied to determine their physiological roles and relation to cell susceptibility.

Identifying characteristics of patients who are more prone to fatal arrhythmias

Using various algorithms to build a machine learning pipeline is the focus of this research. Algorithms like Support Vector Machine, Logistic Regression and Neural Networks were used to exlore the attributes of the shape of action potentials and EKG waveforms and predict patients at high risk of forming an arrhythmia.

Combining mathematical modeling with quantitative physiology

Simulated measures from cardiac myocytes from adult ventricles and induced pluripotent stem cells were used to construct a cross-cell type regression model that predicts adult myocyte drug responses from iPSC-CM behaviors. This cross-cell type model can be of great value in drug development, and the approach, which can be applied to other fields, represents an important strategy for overcoming experimental model limitations.

Itziar Irurzun Arana, PhD

Post Doc

Itziar Irurzun Arana, PhD, is a postdoctoral fellow in Sobielab. She obtained her PhD in Pharmacometrics in the University of Navarra (Pamplona, Spain) under the supervision of Prof. Iñaki Trocóniz. Itziar studied Biomedical Engineering as an undergraduate and has an MSc in Bioinformatics. Within the Cardiac Systems Pharmacology group, she is involved in different projects related to the combination of omics data with modeling (e.g. PredicTox framework) and using the genetic algorithm for parameter estimation in different mechanistic ventricular myocyte models. Her main research interests are in computational modeling (especially in the Systems Pharmacology and Pharmacometrics area) and in optimization techniques.

Taylor Pullinger

PhD Candidate

Taylor joined the PhD in Biomedical Sciences program in 2018 and is part of the Development, Regeneration and Stem Cells (DRS) multidisciplinary training area. She grew up in Johannesburg, South Africa and completed a B.S. in Biology at Brown University before starting at Mount Sinai. In the lab, Taylor focuses on using computational models to study how heterogeneity among cells impacts the resilience of cardiac tissue. Outside the lab, Taylor is an avid squash player and is involved in the New York City biotechnology community through the inter-institutional student-run group GRO-Biotech.
